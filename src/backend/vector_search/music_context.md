# Music Industry Contract Knowledge

## Royalty Hierarchy — CRITICAL
- **Master royalties** are all-encompassing — they cover ALL revenue from exploitation of a master recording.
- If a contract specifies a "master royalty" percentage without listing a specific sub-type separately, that master percentage applies to all sub-types below.
- If a contract specifies a sub-type (e.g. a "streaming split") by name, it IS a master royalty — just stated more specifically.
- **Publishing royalties** (composition/songwriting) are completely SEPARATE from master royalties. Never conflate them.

### Master Royalty Sub-types
The following royalty types are ALL encompassed within master royalties. When any of these are encountered in a contract, treat them as a specific form of master royalty:
- Producer royalty (producer points)
- Sound engineer royalty
- Streaming royalty
- Download royalty
- Physical sales royalty
- Neighboring rights royalty
- Sync licensing royalty
- User-generated content (UGC) royalty
- Digital radio royalty

## Key Distinctions
- **Management commission** (e.g., 20% of gross income) is a cost deducted from the artist's earnings — it is NOT a royalty share. It does not define what the artist earns from streaming or any other source. It defines what the manager takes.
- **Net vs Gross revenue**: Net revenue is calculated after deductions (distribution fees, recoupable costs, etc.). Gross revenue is the total before any deductions. A 25% share of net may yield less than a 20% share of gross in practice. Always note whether a contract specifies net or gross when citing percentages.

## When Answering Questions — MANDATORY
- When citing any master royalty sub-type, always note that it falls under master royalties. Example: "[Artist] has a 25% streaming royalty split ([master royalty sub type] are encompassed within master royalties)"
- If asked about master royalties, look for ANY revenue split matching the sub-types list above — they are all master royalties.
- If a contract says an artist "retains the master rights" AND specifies a sub-type split, that split percentage IS the artist's master royalty share for that revenue stream.
- If asked about a specific sub-type and the contract only specifies "master royalties," the master royalty percentage is the relevant figure — cite it and note it comes from the master royalty clause.
- If asked about "royalties" generically, distinguish which type (master, publishing, mechanical, performance, sync) and cite the specific clause.
